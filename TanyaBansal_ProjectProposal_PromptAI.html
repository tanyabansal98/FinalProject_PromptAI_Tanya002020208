Phase 2 — Uniqueness & The "Special Sauce"

Alright, Show Me What's Under the Hood

Six agents sounds like a lot of buzzwords. What does each one actually do?

Fair skepticism! Think of it like a hospital triage team — one person checks you in, another takes vitals, a nurse assesses severity, a specialist reviews your history, and a coordinator decides where you go next. No single person does everything, and the system works because of that specialization. Here's the pipeline:

1

🔍 Symptom Extractor

Takes messy journal text → extracts structured data: symptom name, body location, severity, duration, frequency, triggers, timestamps. Maps everything to standardized ICD-10-CM codes so doctors can actually use it.

2

📈 Temporal Pattern Analyzer

The "memory" no single LLM has. Tracks symptoms across entries over weeks/months. Detects: escalation (getting worse?), co-occurrence (symptoms appearing together?), cyclical flares, and new emergence.

3

🧬 Condition Cluster Engine (RAG)

Retrieves from medical literature to map symptom clusters → potential condition categories. Not diagnosing — surfacing associations with cited sources. "These symptoms commonly co-occur in [condition] per [PubMed article]."

4

🚨 Severity & Urgency Classifier

Two-axis scoring: Severity (mild→severe based on functional impact) and Urgency (routine→emergency based on clinical red-flag rules from ESI triage guidelines).

5

🏥 Care Pathway Router

The "so what do I actually do?" agent. Maps severity + clusters → care level (ER / urgent / soon / routine) + specialist type (e.g., joint pain + fatigue + rash → rheumatologist).

6

📋 Narrative Summary Generator

Compiles everything into a doctor-ready "Symptom Story" — timeline, patterns, severity trajectory, condition associations with sources, and the patient's own words for context.

Each agent is narrow by design. A single LLM trying to do all six jobs at once will cut corners, hallucinate, and lose the thread. Splitting responsibilities means each step can be tested independently — and when something breaks, you know exactly where.

Okay, but the urgency scoring — is this just vibes, or is there real clinical logic?
"Because 'our AI thinks you're kinda sick' is not a sentence anyone wants to hear."

Ha — exactly. The urgency classifier is grounded in the Emergency Severity Index (ESI), a 5-level triage system actually used in U.S. emergency departments, published by AHRQ. We simplify to four actionable levels:

🟢
Routine

Stable symptoms, no red flags. Mention at next checkup.

🟡
Soon

Worsening pattern. Schedule specialist this week.

🟠
Urgent

Red flags present. See doctor within 24–48 hrs.

🔴
Emergency

Critical combo. ER or 911 now.

Dizzy again. How does the math behind the scoring actually work?

Good — let's get technical for a moment. The urgency score is computed as a composite function:

U(t) = α · R(t) + β · S(t) + γ · Δseverity/Δtime
In plain English: The urgency score U at time t is a weighted combination of three things:

R(t) = Red-flag match score. Binary (0 or 1) — does the current symptom combination match a known emergency pattern? For example, "worst headache of life" + "stiff neck" = 1 (potential subarachnoid hemorrhage). This is rule-based, not learned.

S(t) = Current severity assessment. Scored from functional impact: can the patient work? Sleep? Eat? Walk? Higher functional disruption = higher S.

Δseverity / Δtime = Rate of change. Is the patient getting worse, and how fast? A slow decline over months scores lower than a sharp spike over days.

α, β, γ = Weights. Critically, α is set very high, meaning a red-flag match dominates the score regardless of other factors. Even if the patient writes casually about "a really bad headache, worst ever honestly," the hard-coded red-flag rule fires an emergency override that the LLM cannot suppress.

This is the belt-and-suspenders principle: rule-based red flags + LLM contextual assessment + rate-of-change trending, all in one score. Because in healthcare, you don't gamble on tone detection.
Phase 3 — Measuring Success

Cool Pipeline, But How Do You Know It's Not Hallucinating?

Every AI project says it's "accurate." How are you actually measuring this without lying?

By refusing to hide behind a single end-to-end accuracy number. That's how you lie with AI metrics — you report 92% overall and quietly ignore that your severity classifier is wrong 40% of the time on urgent cases. Instead, each agent gets its own evaluation, so when something breaks, we know exactly which link in the chain failed:

Temporal Acc.

📎
RAG Faithfulness
Every condition claim must trace to a retrieved source document
🤝
Cohen's κ
Triage agreement: system severity scores vs. clinician ratings

Plus: routing accuracy is validated against clinical referral guidelines, and narrative usefulness is evaluated via human audit (would a doctor find this summary helpful? Likert scale on sample outputs). No single metric gets to paper over the others.

Alright, now the scary question. What could go wrong that looks perfectly fine?
"You know — the kind of output that's polished, professional, and quietly dangerous."

These are what I call "silent failures," and they're the ones that keep me honest about this project. Here are the three scariest ones and exactly how the architecture catches each:

⚠️ #1 — The Hallucinated Association
The danger: System says "your cluster matches lupus" — but the RAG actually returned a fibromyalgia document. The LLM invented "lupus" from parametric memory. Looks authoritative. Is wrong.

The catch: Every condition claim must carry a citation ID mapping to a retrieved chunk. A separate faithfulness-checker LLM call compares claim vs. source text. Ungrounded claims get flagged or removed — never silently passed through.
⚠️ #2 — Severity Underestimation
The danger: Patient casually describes "worst headache of my life + stiff neck." Model reads calm tone → scores "moderate." Actual situation: potential subarachnoid hemorrhage. Life-threatening.

The catch: Remember the α weight in U(t)? Hard-coded red-flag keyword rules override LLM scoring. "Worst headache of life" + "neck stiffness" = emergency, regardless of casual tone. The model cannot suppress a rule-based override.
⚠️ #3 — Temporal Hallucination
The danger: Entry 1 mentions headaches, Entry 3 mentions nausea. System reports "nausea began two weeks after headaches" — but the patient never actually stated when nausea started. Looks coherent. Is fabricated.

The catch: Temporal claims get confidence tags. Explicit patient-stated dates = high confidence. Inferred from entry order = medium + visible caveat. No stated date = no claim generated. System prefers "uncertain" over a convincing lie.
🛡️ The Honesty Policy: This system will always prefer displaying "insufficient data" or "uncertain" over generating a confident-looking answer. In healthcare AI, false confidence kills. Admitted uncertainty saves lives.

Okay, but... medical data is notoriously biased. How are you dealing with that?

This is a problem I want to flag early, not sweep under the rug — because acknowledging it honestly is more valuable than pretending it's solved.

⚖️
Bias Acknowledgment

Medical research has historically underrepresented women and minorities. The system explicitly disclaims that pattern matching reflects existing literature and may not capture all population-specific symptom presentations.

🔒
Zero Data Retention

The MVP processes all data within the session. No journal entries are stored, transmitted, or used for training. The patient retains full control over what they share with their doctor.

📖
Full Source Attribution

Every medical claim links to its source — ICD-10 codes, MedlinePlus entries, PubMed papers. Doctors can independently verify every piece of the system's reasoning.

The RAG knowledge base intentionally includes research on health disparities and differential symptom presentation across gender, race, and age. This doesn't eliminate bias — nothing fully does — but it ensures the system isn't blindly amplifying gaps in the literature. And every output explicitly states its limitations, because intellectual honesty is a feature, not a footnote.

One last question on this section. What's the single biggest risk that could tank this whole project?

Hallucination in a healthcare context. Full stop. This isn't an AI writing a bad recipe or a clunky email. A hallucinated condition association could send someone down the wrong medical path. A missed red-flag could cost someone critical hours. Wrong answers here have real consequences — potentially life-and-death ones.

That's precisely why the layered defense (rule-based overrides + RAG faithfulness checking + confidence tagging + "I don't know" as a valid output) isn't a feature bolted on at the end. It's the core architectural decision of the entire project. Everything else is built around it.
